THE HISTORY OF HOME GAMING CONSOLES

By: Nicholas Wijaya

Chapter 1: The Dawn of Console Gaming

Back in the 1950s and 1960s, before modern consoles such as the PS4 or the Xbox One, computer
scientists begun to make simple games on computers, such as MIT's "Spacewar!" in 1962, being the very
first game playable with a video display. However, the first true video game console would made in 1972
by Ralph H. Baer and Magnavox, named the Magnavox Odyssey.


The Beginning of A Magnavox Odyssey

   The Magnavox Odyssey is known for being the very first video game console. It's creator, Ralph H. Baer, got the
idea for games to be able to be played on a TV set while waiting for a colleague at a bus stop. In 1967, he presented a
prototype to his directors at work, and his directors encouraged him to make it something to sell. In 1971, The Magnavox
Odyssey was finally released in retail stores. However, the console was far more expensive than Ralph first envisioned,
despite having attempted to reduce the price as much as possible. Ralph blamed the high price on the Magnavox's "lousy
enginnering", claiming they "over-enginnered" the machine.

   After the release of the Magnavox Odyssey, there were other competitiors, such as the Coleco Telstar, which could
only play video tennis like the Magnavox Odyssey, but it was at half the price of the Magnavox Odyssey, and as such got
far more sales than the Magnavox Odyssey. The Nintendo Color TV Game, Nintendo's first videogame console, sold
around 3 times as much as the Coleco Telstar did. However, the first truly innovative console would only come in late 1976.


Chapter 2: The Second Generation: The Fairchild Channel F and The Impact of The ROM Cartridge

   The Fairchild Channel F was designed by Jerry Lawson and was released by Fairchild Camera
and Instrument in November 1976. It is the first console to use ROM Cartridges, which allowed larger
video games to be ran on consoles, such as the later released Atari 2600 and many other consoles after.
The creators of the ROM Cartridge, Wallace Kirschner and Lawrence Haskel, were also the creators of
a prototype version of the Fairchild Channel F.

   Despite this, the Atari 2600 (branded as the Atari Video Computer System (Atari VCS)) largely
overshadowed the Fairchild Channel F, as it had far better technology than the Fairchild Channel F and
              had better games in general. Despite being $20 more expensive, it had far better video game options than
              the Fairchild Channel F.



Chapter 3: Crash & Burn

   Despite the revolutionizing impact the ROM Cartridge made on the generation and future generations, the crash of the
video game market happened. There were many reasons this happened, from the large amount of consoles competing against each other,
to the large amount of poorly made video games. The computer had also gotten cheaper since the last decade, and people thought
that computers were a better investment than video game consoles in general. The industry wouldn't recover until 1985.

Nintendo: Entertainment System

    The Nintendo Entertainment System (or the Famicom in Japan) was the system that would
save the video game industry in 1985. The unique design that most people think about when asked
to visualize the NES (Nintendo Entertainment System) was designed to look like a video casette
recorder, in order to differentiate the console from past video game consoles due to the recent market
crash. The controller it came with had a D-Pad and 2 face buttons. This layout was used because
Nintendo had to consider that multiple genres of games with different control schemes would be
played on the NES. As such, they had to make a controller that could support most games on the NES.

    The Nintendo Entertainment System is currently the 7th best-selling video game console,
 a surprising feat for a console made in 1983. The NES (Nintedo Entertainment System) is most
                     known as being the console to revitalize the video game market, and showed that consumers still
                     had interest in video games. It is also the console in which many well-known franchises debuted,
                     such as The Legend of Zelda, Mega Man, Castlevania, and many others.


Chapter 4: Competition: Sega's Mega Drive

    The Sega Mega Drive (or The Sega Genesis in the USA) was Sega's answer to Nintendo's
new console, the SNES (Super Nintendo Entertainment System). Despite sales in Japan being
rather poor compared to other consoles at the time, it achieved considerable success in North
America, Brazil, and Europe. There were multiple reasons for this success, from aggresive
youth marketing to the popularity of Sega's Sonic The Hedgehog.


   When Nintendo released the Super Famicom(SNES) in the US, it triggered a fierce battle
between Sega and Nintendo for market shares known as the "console war". The Sega Genesis
                       also faced some controversy from the public, due to violent games such as Mortal Kombat and
                       Night Trap, which resulted in Sega creating the VRC (Videogame Rating Council) which lead
                       to the videogame industry creating the ESRB (Entertainment Software Rating Board)


Chapter 5: The Playstation: Sony Strikes!

   The Playstation entered the videogame market in late competing against the Sega Saturn and the
Nintendo 64. It was also Sony's first attempt at making a videogame console. However, somehow, it far
outsold the Nintendo 64 and Sega Saturn. The Playstation was one of the most notable users of
the new CD-ROM, a new form of game storage first introduced in the 5th generation.
CD-ROMs could store much more data than cartridges, and as such, interested more game developers
to make games for the console.

   The Playstation managed to outsell both the Nintendo 64 and Sega Saturn due to it's
usage of CD-ROMs and it's large 3rd party support. Alongside that, the Playstation gained
                     its success due to a wide variety of popular franchises, such as Castlevania, Final Fantasy,
                     Dragon Quest, and many others, along with popular games such as Crash Bandicoot and
                     Metal Gear Solid.


Chapter 6: Playstation 2: Money moment

   The Playstation 2, released on March 4th, 2000, competed against the Nintendo Gamecube,
the Sega Dreamcast, and Microsoft's first console, the Xbox. As you may know, the Playstation 2 is,
indeed, currently the best-selling video game console of all time. It achieved this due to multiple
factors, one of them being the fact that it was backwards-compatible with the PS1, therfore allowing
consumers with a PS1 to upgrade more affordably and play their old games on the new system. It also
allowed developers time to develop games for the PS2, as most consumers would play their old games
before new games would come out.

   Another reason the Playstation 2 has the title of "Best-selling Videogame Console of All-Time"
was due to the large library of games it had during its lifespan. It had great third-party support, with
games like Final Fantasy and Kingdom Hearts. It was also, surprisingly, marketed as a DVD Player
when it first launched. The Playstation 2 was much more cheaper compared to other DVD players
at the time, so families would buy the PS2 as both a DVD Player and a console.


Chapter 7: Wii: Wii're moving!

   The Wii was released by Nintendo on the 19th of November, 2006. It was underpowered compared to its
competitors, the Playstation 3 and the Xbox 360, but this made its games cheaper to produce. Despite it being
clearly underpowered compared to its competitors, it had backwards compatibility with the previous console,
and a gimmick that made it interesting, Motion Controls.

   The Wii is widely known as being the first console to utillize Motion Controls in its games. The usage
of Motion Controls in some games were considered forced, while others felt right at home, such as Wii Sports,
where the motion controls seemed to be used to their fullest potential. While the Wii definitely wasn't perfect
or had superb processing power, it had very memorable games and its usage of Motion Controls was revolutionary.



In the end, video game consoles will continue to improve over their previous generation's consoles,
and the technology within them will continue to grow.
